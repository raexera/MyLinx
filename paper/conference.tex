\begin{abstract} 

The Micro, Small, and Medium Enterprises (MSME) sector plays a crucial role in the Indonesian economy but faces significant barriers in digital adoption due to the technical complexity of traditional Content Management Systems and the high fees of marketplace platforms. Furthermore, global website builders often lack native integration with local digital habits, such as QRIS payments and WhatsApp communication. This study aims to bridge this gap by developing ”MyLinx” a localized no-code web generator tailored for Indonesian MSMEs using a User-Centered Design (UCD) approach. Initial requirements were gathered from 34 MSME owners, followed by system development and a pilot implementation involving 8 participants. The platform’s usability was evaluated using the System Usability Scale (SUS), yielding an average score of 85.0 (Excellent/Acceptable). The findings indicate that a localized no- code architecture significantly reduces the technical barrier to entry, offering MSMEs an intuitive, cost-effective, and culturally relevant tool to establish a professional digital presence.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The Indonesian digital economy is currently experiencing a period of rapid growth, with projections estimating that its value will reach USD 150 billion by 2025 [1]. Despite this immense potential, the Micro, Small, and Medium Enterprises (MSME) sector—which serves as the backbone of the national economy by contributing 61\% to the Gross Domestic Prod- uct (GDP)—continues to face significant challenges in fully participating in this digital transformation [2]. Research by Rajagukguk and Rusadi (2025) highlights a clear “adoption gap,” revealing that while 65\% of MSMEs recognize the necessity of digital technology, only 23\% are able to utilize it consistently for business operations [3].

This disparity stems largely from a critical gap in technolog- ical compatibility. Currently, MSME owners are often forced into difficult trade-offs between two polar ends of the digital spectrum. On one hand, they adopt marketplace platforms that offer ease of use but impose administrative fees—averaging 6.5\% to 10\%—which significantly erode thin profit margins and limit direct control over customer data [6]. On the other hand, attempting to build independent websites using complex Content Management Systems (CMS) like WordPress often proves too technically demanding for those with limited digital literacy, requiring specialized knowledge in hosting, plugins, and maintenance [8].

Furthermore, existing global website builders such as Wix or Squarespace exhibit a significant \textbf{localization gap}. These plat- forms frequently lack native integration with local Indonesian digital habits, most notably the Unified Quick Response Code Indonesian Standard (QRIS) for payments and automated WhatsApp-based communication, which are primary drivers of commerce in the Indonesian digital ecosystem [18]. While third-party plugins exist, they often introduce additional costs and technical complexity that further alienate non-technical users.

To address these barriers, this research proposes the de- velopment of a localized low-code/no-code web generator platform specifically tailored for the Indonesian MSME land- scape. This platform aims to provide a “middle ground” solution that is intuitive for non-technical users, cost-effective by eliminating commission-based dependencies, and culturally relevant through built-in support for QRIS and WhatsApp.

To evaluate the effectiveness of this solution, the study addresses the following Research Questions (RQ): \textbf{RQ1:} How can a low-code/no-code architecture be designed to integrate localized payment and communication tools while maintaining a minimal learning curve for non-technical users? and \textbf{RQ2:} In what way does the suggested platform help improve the usability, content management, digital visibility, market penetration, and technology adoption among the MSME owners?

The implications of this research are twofold. Firstly, there is the implication in the form of a feasible architecture design for the local web generation service that will close the gap between user-friendliness and professionalism. Secondly, there is the implication in the form of an empirical evaluation based on the System Usability Scale (SUS). It allows one to see the benchmark of how the localization of no-code platforms may be instrumental in facilitating access to digital technologies for small businesses in developing countries.

\section{Literature Review}

\subsection{Digital Transformation and Website Adoption for MSMEs}
Micro, Small, and Medium Enterprises (MSMEs) contribute significantly to economic growth, especially in developing countries, by employing people and adding to GDP [4]. In addition to the development of digital technology, MSMEs have been encouraged to embrace digital solutions in order to enhance efficiency in communication, marketing, and business. Digital transformation allows organizations to leverage digital technologies in optimizing their communication and marketing as well as streamlining their business activities [5].

However, despite these advantages, the uptake of digital technology is low amongst MSMEs because of inadequate digital literacy skills, financial challenges, and lack of technical expertise [6]. Some MSMEs only use social networking sites as a means of promoting their services digitally. Even though this is convenient, it limits their identity development and prevents them from owning data about customers.

The web sites provide a better digital appearance for MSMEs as they help the business showcase their product catalogues, services, and business details on its own. The business website also helps in gaining customer trust and developing better digital branding approaches [7]. But web sites often demand technical expertise related to setting up the host server, website management, and interface design, making it tough for nontechnical individuals to work on them [8]. Thus, MSMEs need a website portal that is user-friendly and easily manageable without requiring any coding skills.

\subsection{No-Code Platforms and User-Centered Design}
With the rise of no-code and low-code software, web and app creation has become easier for those without technical skills. The no-code system provides tools that enable users to create applications and web pages without having to write any computer code [9].

Low-code and no-code platforms are increasingly adopted because they reduce development complexity and shorten implementation time [10]. Several platforms such as Word- Press and Wix provide website-building services with varying levels of flexibility. However, these platforms may still present usability challenges for MSME owners with limited technical experience. In addition, support for local business needs such as WhatsApp communication and QRIS payment integration is often limited.

To improve usability and user acceptance, the development of digital systems should involve users throughout the design process. User-Centered Design (UCD) is an approach that focuses on user needs, characteristics, and experiences during system development [11]. UCD aims to improve usability, accessibility, and user satisfaction by involving users during requirement gathering, design evaluation, and usability testing [12]. Through continuous user evaluation, UCD helps ensure that the developed platform is more relevant and easier to use for target users.

\subsection{System Usability Scale (SUS)}
Usability is an important aspect of digital platform devel- opment because it influences user satisfaction, efficiency, and ease of use [13]. Systems with good usability are more likely to be accepted and continuously used by users

One of the most widely used methods for usability eval- uation is the System Usability Scale (SUS), introduced by Brooke [16]. SUS consists of ten questionnaire items used to evaluate users’ perceptions of system usability. The method is considered simple, reliable, and effective for evaluating websites, applications, and digital platforms [17]. SUS is com- monly used because it provides a quick usability assessment while remaining easy for participants to understand.

In this study, SUS is used to evaluate the usability of the MyLinx platform after being tested by MSME owners. The evaluation results are expected to measure how easily users can operate and adapt to the developed platform.

\subsection{Research Gap}
Previous studies have discussed digital transformation and website adoption among MSMEs. However, existing platforms generally focus either on flexibility with higher technical complexity or simplicity with limited customization and lo- calization.

Most marketplace platforms provide ease of use but limit business identity development and customer data control. On the other hand, website development platforms such as WordPress and Wix provide higher flexibility but still require technical understanding that may be difficult for MSME own- ers with low digital literacy.

In addition, previous studies rarely focus on developing a no-code website platform specifically designed for MSMEs
 
with integrated local business features such as WhatsApp com- munication and QRIS payment systems while simultaneously evaluating usability and technology adaptation among non- technical users. Therefore, this research aims to develop a user- friendly no-code website platform for MSMEs using a User- Centered Design approach and evaluate its usability using the System Usability Scale (SUS).

\section{Research Methodology}
\subsection{Research Design}
This study employed a Research and Development (R\&D) methodology combined with a User-Centered Design (UCD) approach to develop the MyLinx platform for MSMEs. The research focused on identifying user needs, developing a no- code website platform, and evaluating its usability and impact on MSME digital adoption.


\subsection{Requirement Gathering}
In the beginning, challenges associated with the creation and management of MSME businesses' websites were established. Data collection was performed using a pre-development survey distributed among MSME business owners. A Likert scale was used in the survey in order to establish MSME business owners' views on the significance of having a website, interest in no-code website platform, digital adaption ability, need for website management and the use of WhatsApp and QRIS functionalities.

There is widespread application of the Likert scale technique in the study of user perceptions and usability because it allows for establishing the degree of agreement with certain statements from the part of respondents [14].

\subsection{System Development}
The MyLinx platform is a result of the user needs identified during the process of its creation and represents a no-code website builder targeted at MSMEs. The features that can be found in the platform include customizable templates, product catalog management, integration with WhatsApp, QRIS payment systems, and a more convenient dashboard. In order to develop the system, Laravel framework and MVC architecture, along with the use of Blade template engine and TailwindCSS were used. VPS deployment environment included such services as Nginx, PHP-FPM, and SSL/TLS protection.

\subsection{System Implementation}

Following the completion of the development process, the platform was subsequently implemented and tested by MSME owners over a period of two weeks. At this point, participants utilized the platform independently to build their websites for their businesses. The purpose of implementing the platform was to observe how users interact with the platform in real life situations and adapt to the new system.

The MyLinx platform supports both centralized hosting and independent self-deployment. To deploy the system, users must prepare a Linux-based server or Virtual Private Server (VPS) with Git, Docker, Docker Compose v2, and GNU Make installed. These components provide the necessary environ- ment for source code management, container orchestration, and automated deployment processes.

The source code, installation guide, and deployment resources are publicly available through the official GitHub repository at https://github.com/raexera/MyLinx. Users, MSMEs, and organizations can clone the repository and deploy their own isolated instance by following the provided installation documentation.

A live production deployment of MyLinx is accessible at https://mylinx.tech/. This publicly available instance serves as a reference implementation and demonstrates the platform operating in a real-world production environment.

\subsection{Post-Implementation Evaluation}

After the implementation process, the participants were asked to complete a questionnaire aimed at measuring the effectiveness of the MyLinx platform. Some of the parameters included in the questionnaire included content management, increased reach in the market, improved visibility, tech adaptation, ease of use, and satisfaction levels.

All questionnaire items were measured using a five-point Likert scale ranging from strongly disagree to strongly agree. The evaluation results were used to analyze user perceptions regarding the effectiveness of the developed platform.

\subsection{Usability Evaluation Using SUS}

System usability evaluation was conducted using the System Usability Scale (SUS) proposed by Brooke [16]. SUS consists of ten standardized statements used to measure users’ percep- tions regarding the usability of a system.

SUS is widely used because it provides a simple, reliable, and efficient usability evaluation method for digital systems [17]. Participants completed the SUS questionnaire after using the platform during the implementation phase. The SUS score was calculated using the standard SUS scoring method to determine the usability level and user acceptance of the MyLinx platform.

\subsection{Data Analysis}

The collected data were analyzed using descriptive quan- titative analysis. Responses from both pre-implementation and post-implementation questionnaires were calculated using mean scores and percentage analysis to evaluate user percep- tions toward the developed platform.

In addition, the SUS evaluation results were analyzed to determine the overall usability performance of the system. Higher SUS scores indicated better usability and stronger user acceptance of the developed platform.

\subsection{System Usability Scale (SUS) Analysis}

The usability evaluation of the MyLinx platform was con- ducted using the System Usability Scale (SUS) method involv- ing 8 MSME participants. The SUS questionnaire consisted of 10 statements designed to measure system usability, ease of use, learnability, and user satisfaction.

Each questionnaire item was measured using a five-point Likert scale ranging from strongly disagree to strongly agree. The SUS score calculation followed the standard SUS formula, where odd-numbered items were reduced by 1 and even- numbered items were subtracted from 5. The total score was then multiplied by 2.5 to obtain the final SUS score for each respondent. The SUS calculation results are presented in Table \ref{tab:sus_result}.

Based on Table IV, the MyLinx platform obtained an average SUS score of 85.0. According to the SUS interpre- tation scale, this score falls into the Excellent category and is classified as Acceptable. This result indicates that the platform has a high level of usability and can be easily used by MSME users without requiring technical or coding skills.

\section{Conclusion}
MyLinx platform was successfully created and assessed as a localized, no-code web generation solution that allows accelerating digital transformation among Indonesian MSMEs. In relation to research question 1, the platform confirmed that a low-code/no-code architecture can be easily integrated into a local business toolset, namely the usage of WhatsApp and QRIS services while excluding the necessity of coding. In connection with research question 2, the positive results were observed in terms of product management and digital exposure, evidenced by the System Usability Scale score of 85.0 and the ”Excellent” classification of platform usability.

While achieving a satisfactory level of success, the current study identifies one major limitation in terms of the sample size utilized in the post-implementation phase. The limited number of participants was represented by only eight MSME owners who have used the system for evaluation purposes, limiting generalization of quantitative business impact indicators (e.g., improvement of market penetration rate). Further research is therefore aimed at scaling the MyLinx platform implementation among Indonesian MSMEs.
